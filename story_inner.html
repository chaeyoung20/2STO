Overseas Remote Work Challenge - Thailand

Alina Co., Ltd

✔ ︎ Note: Ben of the Slowwalk IF team has transferred the article that was published in the team blog.

At the start of 2018, the IF team has planned a new project. It is remote working overseas where the entire team leaves, and the region is a little unfamiliar with the Thai resort of Ko Samui. As it is known, there are employees who work remotely in Slowwalk. Depending on the nature of the work, I and other team members also often work at home or in cafes if necessary. One of the welfare systems of Sloobark is called the 'sacrifice month.' You're given 30 days of paid leave for every two years on the job, and Keaton, the leader of Team IF, is back. That's how it works Keaton's family went on a trip in time for the Sabbath, and two members, including myself, came to plan the Ko Samui project together in the name of remote work.

Ko Samui is an island about 1/8 of Jeju Island, and I used it a lot because there were many restaurants and cafes like this on the beach. Being able to sit in this place and work while listening to the sound of the waves is the most important I think it was a big advantage. Of course, the fact that we have to work ahead of the ocean is a downside at the same time. We each go to the beaches and cafes we want to visit separately and work there, and a good place to go would be the best If there was, we would share it and see each other. Of course, there were days when we just hung out. Thailand is already a country that many digital nomads are looking for, and I have firsthand felt that low prices and high Internet penetration play a big part. Even within this small island, cafes are 100 percent, Even at regular restaurants, Wi-Fi was available frequently. And the SIM card that can be purchased from telecommunication companies was also available at a very low price compared to the price in Korea. It seems that I had to participate in regular meetings three to four times a week, but I was surprised that Google Hangout provided much more pleasant usability than expected. There was a power outage about once a week, but it was okay to go to the next neighborhood, so it wasn't too uncomfortable. Considering the harsh weather in Seoul, which is more than 30 degrees Celsius apart, nothing is uncomfortable here No, I don't.


I stayed in Bangkok for a few days away from my team. Perhaps because it's one of the cities that nomads actually visit a lot, it was also a place with a lot of hip places. There are various concept co-working places, and most of all, there are so many pretty cafes. Perhaps because of the large land, the cafes were generally large and relaxed. Most of them are expensive compared to the prices in Thailand, but they are also inexpensive compared to Korea, so it's not burdensome. Even if you eat well, you can solve both a meal and a cup of coffee at around 10,000 won. Here's a little more listing of the characteristics of Thailand/Bangkok that I felt as a remote worker. It's not a good place to walk. Cars and bikes travel all the way to the alleys, so there is a lot of noise and traffic congestion. It was the biggest drawback I felt. I rented a bike in Koh Samui, and I was scared in Bangkok, so I usually used a taxi and subway. The food is delicious. As it is known, street food has developed a lot and the price is very affordable. Your taste may work, but I thought Thailand would be better than Korea if you only looked at the menu you eat alone. There are so many white people everywhere. There was a moment of distracting thinking about whether it was white people who led tourism or anything. They're relaxed. I guess because I'm Korean, it's inevitable. Most people are relaxed and kind, so I felt softened as well.
